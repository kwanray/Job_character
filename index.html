Theodicy

The Problem of Evil

Why does a good, all-powerful God allow suffering? This is the question at the heart of Job — and at the heart of Norman Geisler's life work as a philosopher and theologian.

"The problem of evil is not whether God exists in spite of evil, but whether there is a satisfying explanation of how God and evil can both exist." — Norman Geisler, The Roots of Evil

The problem of evil takes two forms. The logical problem (posed by philosopher J.L. Mackie) argues that an all-good, all-powerful God is logically incompatible with evil's existence — if God could prevent evil and wanted to, he would. The evidential problem (posed by William Rowe) is softer: even if God and evil aren't logically incompatible, the sheer scale and intensity of suffering makes God's existence unlikely.

Job faces both. His suffering raises the logical question (why didn't God protect a blameless man?) and the evidential question (what purpose could this level of loss possibly serve?). The book doesn't dodge these questions — it dramatises them with unsparing honesty.

Geisler argues that moral evil — suffering caused by human choices — flows from genuine freedom. A God who creates beings capable of real love must create beings capable of real choice. Genuine choice includes the possibility of wrong choices. Wrong choices produce harm.

This doesn't explain all evil in Job (his disasters include natural catastrophes), but it explains why a good God might create a world with suffering potential at all. The capacity for suffering is the shadow side of the capacity for love.

Geisler maintains that God never causes evil but may permit evil when it serves a greater purpose that could not otherwise be achieved. The distinction matters: God is not the author of suffering, but he is sovereign over it.

In Job, God permits the adversary's challenge not because he is cruel, but because Job's faithfulness under fire will demonstrate something significant: that disinterested, genuine faith exists. The greater good — the vindication of faithful love — required the test.

Drawing on the Irenaean tradition, Geisler acknowledges that suffering can be the necessary context for the formation of virtues that cannot develop in a pain-free world. Courage requires real danger. Patience requires real frustration. Compassion requires real pain.

Elihu, the youngest character in Job, articulates a version of this: God uses suffering as a form of communication — to turn people from destructive paths and refine their character. Not all suffering is punitive; some is formative.

Geisler insists that ultimate theodicy awaits eternity. The final account is not closed in this life. God will right every wrong; suffering that appears purposeless in the present will look different in light of what comes after.

Job's restoration in chapter 42 — double what he lost — is a foretaste of this. It is not meant to suggest that all suffering ends in material prosperity, but that God's ultimate intention toward his people is redemptive. The last word belongs to God, not to suffering.

Geisler argues that every worldview must face the problem of evil — but not every worldview can adequately answer it. His comparative analysis, developed across The Roots of Evil and If God, Why Evil?, shows why Christianity stands alone in offering a coherent and complete response.

Atheism

Position: There is no God; evil and suffering are simply facts of a blind, purposeless universe.

The problem: Atheism cannot even define evil as truly evil without borrowing a moral standard it cannot justify. Without a transcendent God, there is no objective basis for calling anything wrong — only personal preference or social consensus. The atheist uses evil as an argument against God, yet without God the word "evil" loses its force. And crucially, there is no one to answer — no being with the power or will to address, redeem, or ultimately end suffering.

New Age & Pantheism

Position: God is all and all is God (the impersonal Absolute). Evil and suffering are ultimately illusions — products of ignorance or false perception (maya). Enlightenment dissolves the illusion.

The problem: If evil is merely an illusion, why does the illusion cause real pain? An impersonal cosmic force cannot care about suffering, cannot enter it, and cannot resolve it. The "solution" — realising evil isn't real — offers nothing to someone in genuine agony. As Geisler notes, pantheism affirms God but denies evil; this is not a solution but a denial of the very problem it is supposed to address.

Buddhism

Position: Suffering (dukkha) arises from desire and attachment. Past karma — wrong actions across previous lives — shapes present experience. The solution is to extinguish desire and achieve nirvana: the cessation of craving, and ultimately of self.

The problem: There is no personal God who enters history to help, no atonement for moral failure, and no resurrection. The goal is escape from existence, not redemption of it. Suffering is explained by prior karma, but the sufferer is offered no advocate, no forgiveness, and no guarantee that the cycle will end. Buddhism acknowledges the reality of suffering but has no personal God to address it.

Christianity — The Unique Answer

Position: A real, personal God exists. Evil is also genuinely real — not an illusion. God neither ignores evil nor explains it away. He enters it.

In the incarnation, God became flesh and experienced suffering first-hand. In the crucifixion, God bore the full weight of evil's consequences — not as a bystander but as the victim. In the resurrection, God defeated evil's ultimate power over life. In the new creation, God promises to end evil's story entirely. Christianity alone, Geisler argues, affirms a real God who faces real evil and provides a real solution — not through denial, not through escape, but through redemption.

Job's story anticipates this pattern: suffering is real, God is real, and the last word — restoration — belongs to God.

Biblical Analysis

Geisler's Exposition of Job

Norman Geisler reads Job as a carefully structured theological drama. Understanding its architecture is the key to interpreting its message correctly.

Ch. 1–2

Prose Prologue

The heavenly court. God and the adversary. Job's character established as "blameless and upright." Two waves of catastrophe strip Job of wealth, children, and health. The dramatic irony: the reader knows what Job does not — this is a test of disinterested faith.

Ch. 3–31

Three Cycles of Dialogue

Three rounds of speeches between Job and his friends (Eliphaz, Bildad, Zophar). Each round escalates in intensity. The friends grow harsher; Job grows more confident in his innocence and his demand to meet God. By round three, Zophar has nothing left to say.

Ch. 32–37

Elihu's Speeches

The young Elihu has waited respectfully. Now he speaks — rebuking both the friends (for condemning Job wrongly) and Job (for implying God is unjust). His key contribution: suffering can be instructive, not only punitive. He is a theological bridge to what follows.

Ch. 38–41

God Speaks from the Whirlwind

Two divine speeches. Not an explanation of Job's suffering — but 77+ rhetorical questions about creation, cosmos, and creature. "Where were you when I laid the foundations of the earth?" Job is invited into God's perspective. The crisis resolves — not through argument but through encounter.

Ch. 42

Prose Epilogue

Job's transformation: "My ears had heard of you, but now my eyes have seen you." God rebukes the three friends for speaking wrongly about him. Job intercedes for them. Job is restored — double his former possessions. Faith vindicated. Suffering was not the last word.

Why the Friends Were Wrong

Geisler's analysis centres on a critical theological error: the three friends took a generally true principle and applied it mechanically to a specific person without justification.

⚖️

The Deuteronomic Assumption

They assumed the covenant principle of Deuteronomy 28 (blessings for obedience, curses for disobedience) applies directly and immediately to every individual in every circumstance. This confuses a national covenant promise with a universal algorithm for personal fate.

🔄

Reasoning Backwards

They reasoned from effect to cause: "You are suffering, therefore you sinned." This commits the logical fallacy of affirming the consequent. Even if sin always produced suffering, it would not follow that all suffering proves sin.

🗡️

Theology as Weapon

They came to console but became accusers. Geisler notes the pastoral tragedy: they deployed correct theological categories (God's holiness, human sinfulness) as weapons against a suffering man, rather than as comfort for him.

📜

God's Direct Verdict

"You have not spoken of me what is right, as my servant Job has." (Job 42:7) God rebukes the friends explicitly. Job's angry, honest wrestling was more righteous before God than the friends' polished, confident orthodoxy. Right theology wrongly applied is still theological error.

Elihu: The Transitional Voice

Elihu is the book's most intriguing character — present in chapters 32–37, absent from the epilogue's rebuke, and often overlooked in popular readings of Job.

🤫

The Younger Who Waited

Elihu respects the elders' right to speak first. When they run out of answers, he steps in — rebuking the friends for failing to refute Job and rebuking Job for implying God had been unjust to him.

🎓

Suffering as Instruction

Elihu's theological advance: suffering is not only retrospective punishment but proleptic instruction. God may use hardship to speak before a person goes further down a destructive path — to warn, redirect, and keep them from worse harm. (Job 33:14–18)

✅

Not Rebuked in the Epilogue

Crucially, God does not rebuke Elihu in chapter 42 — only the three friends are condemned. Geisler interprets this as a sign that Elihu's theology, while incomplete, is directionally correct. He represents a genuine step beyond simple retribution theology.

🌉

Bridge to the Divine Speeches

Elihu's final speech closes with language about God speaking through creation — "God thunders wondrously with his voice" — which directly prepares for the voice from the whirlwind in chapter 38. He is a theological bridge, not a dead end.

The Answer from the Whirlwind

Chapters 38–41 are among the most dramatic passages in all of Scripture. God finally speaks — but not to answer Job's questions directly.

🌪️

Questions, Not Answers

God's response consists of 77+ rhetorical questions. He does not explain the cosmic wager. He does not justify Job's suffering. Instead, he expands Job's frame of reference entirely: "Where were you when I laid the foundation of the earth?" (Job 38:4)

🌌

A Bigger View of God

Geisler's central interpretation: the real problem was not that Job lacked answers — it was that Job lacked an adequate vision of God. The whirlwind speech provides that vision. The encounter itself resolves the crisis. Not argument, but presence.

🐉

Behemoth and Leviathan

God's second speech focuses on two chaos-creatures beyond human control. The point: even the most terrifying forces in creation are under God's governance. Chaos itself is not outside his sovereignty. If God manages Leviathan, he can manage Job's suffering.

👁️

Job's Transformation

"My ears had heard of you, but now my eyes have seen you." (42:5) Job's response is not "Now I understand why I suffered." It is "Now I have seen you." The resolution is personal encounter, not intellectual explanation — Geisler's central interpretive claim about the whole book.

Application

What Job Means for You

Written for Christian teenagers in Singapore — but if you're wrestling with suffering, pressure, and faith, this is for you too. Job's questions are timeless.

📚

Academic Pressure

PSLE. O-Levels. A-Levels. The weight of an education system built on performance — and the nagging fear that a bad result means God is displeased with you.

What Job teaches: Job lost everything he had built — and it was not because of any failure on his part. His friends assumed it was. They were wrong, and God rebuked them for it. A hard season does not mean you are being punished.

Not all suffering is punishment. Lament is allowed. Bring your anxiety to God honestly — that is exactly what Job did, and God called it more righteous than polished, performed faith.

"Do not be anxious about anything, but in everything by prayer... present your requests to God." — Philippians 4:6

Family Expectations

In Singapore's family culture, the weight of parental sacrifice is real. Disappointing your parents can feel like a moral failure — even a spiritual one.

What Job teaches: Job's integrity was his own — not derived from his social standing or family reputation. When everything was stripped away, he had to know who he was before God, not before others. His worth did not change with his circumstances.

Your identity before God is not defined by your family's honour or your performance. Geisler's soul-making principle: pressure is the context in which real character is formed — not destroyed.

"Am I now trying to win the approval of human beings, or of God?" — Galatians 1:10

Faith Questions & Doubt

University, secular peers, social media. Questions about science, suffering, and whether Christianity holds up. The pressure to perform certainty you don't actually feel.

What Job teaches: Job asks the hardest questions — out loud, directly at God. God does not rebuke him for asking. He rebukes the friends who gave confident, tidy answers instead. Honest wrestling is more faithful than performed certainty.

Your questions are not disqualifying. Doubt taken honestly to God is itself an act of faith. Geisler: the encounter with God — not the resolution of every question — is what transforms us.

"Though he slay me, I will hope in him; yet I will argue my ways to his face." — Job 13:15

🌑

Suffering Without Answers

Mental health struggles, broken families, loss, grief. Pain that doesn't make sense — and a church culture that sometimes demands you explain it or smile through it.

What Job teaches: God's answer to Job was not an explanation — it was a presence. The whirlwind did not resolve Job's suffering intellectually; it gave him an encounter with the living God. That was enough.

You don't need to resolve your suffering intellectually before God can meet you in it. The God who governs Leviathan governs your pain too. Geisler: the final resolution may lie beyond what we can see now — but it is real.

"Our present sufferings are not worth comparing with the glory that will be revealed." — Romans 8:18

Watch Who You Listen To

Prosperity gospel preachers, influencers with spiritual frameworks for every problem, and well-meaning friends who have confident answers to why you're struggling.

What Job teaches: Job's friends came with theological systems and used them to wound him. They gave confident answers to questions they didn't understand. God rebuked them for it. Not all pastoral advice is godly advice — even when it sounds correct.

The prosperity gospel — "be faithful and God will bless you materially" — is exactly the friends' error. Test what you hear. God vindicated Job's honest struggle over the friends' tidy orthodoxy.

"You have not spoken of me what is right, as my servant Job has." — Job 42:7

Identity & Resilience

When everything is stripped away — results, reputation, approval — who are you? What remains when the externals are gone?

What Job teaches: Job was "blameless and upright" before his suffering, and God restores him after. His identity was not defined by his prosperity. The adversary believed removing the blessings would remove the faith. He was wrong.

You are not your grades, your CCA record, your social standing, or your family's expectations. Geisler: disinterested faith — loving God for who he is, not what he gives — is the deepest resilience there is. Job proved it exists.

"Does Job fear God for nothing?" — Job 1:9 (The accuser asked. The answer was yes.)
